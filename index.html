Why MIA Exists

The bridge between
care and coverage.

Up to $0 per pregnancy is set aside to cover doula care. Most of it goes unclaimed. Not because the care didn’t happen, but because documentation breaks down before a claim ever reaches the payer. In Oklahoma, SoonerCare funds more than 0 births a year. The care is consistent. The paper trail is where it falls apart.

Total transparency.

Payers already fund this care, and the reimbursement is there. What went missing was the accountability between the people providing care and the plans paying for it. MIA closes that gap with clean, visit-level records that both sides can verify and stand behind.

For Doulas

Speak freely.
MIA does the rest.

After a visit, open MIA and just talk for about 90 seconds, in your own words. MIA checks what you said against your state’s requirements and builds a clean, submission-ready record. It stays in your voice and becomes a lasting record of the care you gave. Starting in Oklahoma, that record is what gets you paid.

“I love this. The doulas I mentor could really benefit from something like this. Documentation is one of the top challenges we have with them.”
Jacquelyn Clemmons, Systems Change Advocate, South Carolina
Just Talk
Speak naturally after your visit. No templates, no checkboxes, no clinical words required. MIA understands how doulas talk about their work.
Checked As You Go
Every note is matched against Oklahoma’s SoonerCare rules. You see what is complete and what is missing before you close the app.
Private by Design
No client names needed to record. MIA handles your audio without personal details and adds identifiers only at the secure, HIPAA-safe export.
One-Tap Record
A submission-ready record in seconds. One tap and it is formatted, complete, and ready for billing.

Built by someone who was in that room.

Mia Woodson is a U.S. Army veteran, mother of four, and has called Oklahoma home for nearly twenty years. In the Army, she coordinated large-scale training operations across complex logistics systems, where every person, supply, and timeline had to be accounted for with precision. Years later, as a practicing birth doula in Oklahoma, she watched that same discipline go missing in the one system that needed it most.

The care was happening every day. The documentation, coordination, and pay were not keeping pace. It was not a failure of effort. It was a structural gap. So she built PDI, applying the operational rigor she developed in the Army to a corner of perinatal care that had gone underdocumented for far too long.
